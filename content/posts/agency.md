## Agency

A principal engineer at a growing startup has spent three years watching the same architectural mistake compound itself. She understands the fix. She has the seniority. She has access to decision-makers. She does not act.

She is not lazy or incompetent. She has stopped believing that her actions will change anything. Three previous proposals went nowhere. The organization absorbed her effort and returned nothing. Without intending to, it taught her that acting is not worth it.

Three weeks later, a junior engineer on the same team pulls up the same architecture diagram, writes a one-page proposal, and schedules fifteen minutes with the VP of Engineering. The proposal succeeds. Not because of superior capability. Because of agency.

## What Is Agency?

Bandura (1989) defines human agency as the capacity to originate and direct actions for given purposes. Three properties constitute it: intentionality, forethought, and self-reactiveness.

These properties distinguish agency from related concepts. Autonomy is the freedom to act. Agency is the exercise of that freedom. Motivation is the desire to act. Agency converts desire into sustained, directed behavior. Potential describes what a person could accomplish. Agency is what closes the gap between potential and outcome.

Agency is also not the same as competence. A highly competent person who acts only when directed, or only within already-defined boundaries, is not exercising agency. She is executing.

Autonomy, motivation, and potential can coexist with the absence of agency. The principal engineer has all three. What the organization dismantled, over time, is something more foundational: the belief that her actions matter.

## The Structure of Agency

**Intentionality** is the foundation. It requires more than preference. It requires committing to a specific future state and forming a plan to reach it (Bandura, 1989). The engineer who privately wants things to change but has not made an explicit commitment to a plan has not yet exercised agency. She has exercised preference.

**Forethought** extends agency across time. An agent does not merely react to the present. She anticipates future states, sets goals in relation to them, and acts now on behalf of outcomes that do not yet exist. Acting on behalf of a mental model of the future is what separates directed behavior from simple response (Bandura, 1989). It is also what makes agency strategic. The agent chooses where to direct effort, not just whether to direct it at all. Without forethought, even strong intentionality produces motion without trajectory.

**Self-reactiveness** sustains agency through setbacks. An agent monitors her own behavior, compares it against her intentions, and adjusts. Without self-reactiveness, intentions erode into distraction and goals become wishes. Self-reactiveness is the loop that keeps agency alive across time, not only at the moment of initial commitment.

Together, these properties explain why agency is more than willpower. Willpower is force applied at a single moment. Agency is the structure of intention, foresight, and self-correction that sustains directed behavior over time.

## What Blocks Agency

Seligman (1972) documented the external mechanism that degrades agency most reliably: learned helplessness. When people experience repeated uncontrollable outcomes, situations where their actions produce no effect, they stop trying. The pattern persists even after conditions change. People who have learned that action is futile do not immediately resume acting when futility ends. The belief outlasts the evidence.

Organizations create learned helplessness at scale without intending to. Proposals that die without feedback. Strategic pivots that abandon completed work without explanation. Approval processes that reliably say no. Leaders who request input and consistently disregard it. Each incident is small. The cumulative signal is not.

Ryan and Deci (2000) identify the conditions that sustain agency from the inside: autonomy (the experience of genuine choice), competence (the experience of being effective), and relatedness (the sense of mattering to others affected by your actions). When these conditions are present, people form intentions, sustain effort, and regulate their own behavior toward goals. When any one is stripped away, agency contracts. Competence without autonomy produces technically skilled people who wait to be assigned. Autonomy without competence produces motion without direction. Relatedness is the overlooked factor: people act more persistently when they believe their actions matter to others, not just to themselves.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing busyness with agency.** A full calendar and a closed task list is not agency. It is reaction. Agency requires knowing what you are trying to accomplish and orienting behavior toward that outcome.

**Treating autonomy as sufficient.** Having freedom to act is not the same as exercising agency. Many people in high-autonomy roles remain passive. Autonomy is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one.

**Waiting for certainty.** Agency acts in conditions of incomplete information. Waiting for the right moment, better data, or unambiguous permission is usually the suspension of agency dressed as prudence.

**Conflating potential with performance.** High potential and low agency is one of the most common patterns in technical organizations. The person with the deepest understanding of what needs to change is often the least likely to act on it. Insight without intention is not agency.

**Attributing learned helplessness to character.** When people on your team stop proposing, stop committing, stop taking ownership, that is most often a learned response to the environment, not a reflection of who they are. The history of what happened to previous attempts predicts future agentic behavior more reliably than any stated value.

## Put It Into Practice

Agency is not a personality trait. It is a practice, and it erodes when the conditions that sustain it are absent.

For yourself: identify one outcome you care about that has not moved in more than a month. Not a task. An outcome. Write a specific intention toward it and one action you will take this week that requires no approval. The point is not the action itself. It is restoring the habit of forming intentions and following through.

For managers: examine the last three times a direct report's initiative went nowhere. What happened to the proposal? What happened to the person afterward? That sequence is the environment you have built. If you want more agency from your team, the leverage point is restoring the belief that action determines outcomes. That cannot be done with a values statement. It requires consistent, visible follow-through on proposals that deserve it.
