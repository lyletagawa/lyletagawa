##

`${EMPLOYER}` recently conducted a semi-annual employee survey. Many expected topics were surveyed including career development, inclusion, job satisfaction, and manager effectiveness.

Some employees provide harsh feedback. They might imagine that their honest criticism leads to constructive reflection and change in the organization.

Some employees provide pleasant feedback. They might imagine that their honest criticism leads to retalliation.

Based on that observable behavior alone, what can we infer about that employee? Do cheerful optimists make the best employees? Do prickly contrarians make the worst employees?

## Givers and Takers

> Can you give me four names of people whose careers you have fundamentally improved?
> Takers will give you four names who are all more influential than them
> Takers are great at kissing up and then kicking down
> Givers are more likely to name people who are below them in a hierarchy, who don't have as much power, who can do them no good

## No Asshole Rule

> a more vulgar name for disagreeable takers

## Challenging people without being a jerk

> Radically candid criticism is both clear and kind. You don’t soften the truth but don’t want to hurt the person either.

> Brutal honesty is about getting everything out of the system without caring about others. We end doing more harm than good.

> Kim Scott refers to brutal honesty as “front stabbing.” We are looking at people in the eye, but still hurting them.

```
         Ruinous Empathy | Radical Candor
-------------------------+---------------------
Manipulative Insincerity | Obnoxious Aggression
```

> Radical candor is not:
> 
>     An opportunity to be gratuitously harsh
>     A chance to “front-stab”
>     An excuse to nitpick

Even when a survey is anonymous, it may not be the best way to practice radical candor.

You do not establish a personal relationship (with the survey). You are both praising and criticizing in public.
