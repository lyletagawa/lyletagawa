## The People Closest to the Work

The night before the Challenger launch, engineer Roger Boisjoly is on a conference call telling Morton Thiokol's management the O-rings might fail in the cold. He has data on it. He has seen erosion on these seals before, on warmer launches than this one{{< cite 1 "Roger Boisjoly. Wikipedia." >}}.

Management asks the engineers to prove the O-rings will fail. Nobody can prove a negative on a conference call at 11pm. The managers go back on the line without their own engineers present and reverse the recommendation to launch{{< cite 1 "Roger Boisjoly. Wikipedia." >}}. The next morning, Challenger breaks apart 73 seconds after liftoff.

Boisjoly wasn't ignored because he was wrong. He was ignored because the people making the final call were several layers removed from the data he was looking at, and their incentives ran the other way.

## What Deming Actually Argued

W. Edwards Deming spent his career arguing a version of this same point, long before Challenger. In Out of the Crisis, he lays out 14 points for management, and a startling number of them boil down to the same instruction: stop overriding the people doing the work with targets, slogans, and fear{{< cite 2 "Deming, W. Edwards (1986). Out of the Crisis. MIT Press." >}}.

Point 8 is "drive out fear," on the theory that a worker afraid of the consequences of reporting a problem will simply stop reporting it. Points 11a and 11b eliminate quotas and management by numerical objective, because a target imposed from above tells a worker nothing about how to actually improve the process, only that they'll be blamed for missing it. Point 10 goes after slogans and exhortations directly, noting that most causes of low quality sit in the system itself, beyond any individual worker's power to fix by trying harder{{< cite 2 "Deming, W. Edwards (1986). Out of the Crisis. MIT Press." >}}.

Strip the numbering away and the argument is simple. The system produces the results. The people running the system, day after day, are the ones who can see where it's broken. Management's job is to remove what's in their way, not to install a scoreboard and hope.

## Why Distance Destroys Information

Every layer between the work and the decision filters something out. A line worker sees a specific defect on a specific unit. A supervisor sees a weekly summary. A director sees a monthly chart. By the time a number reaches the person with authority to act on it, the texture that made the original problem obvious is gone.

Sociologist Diane Vaughan spent years reconstructing exactly how this played out at NASA before Challenger. Engineers watched O-ring erosion happen, flight after flight, and each time it didn't cause a disaster, the same amount of erosion got quietly reclassified as an acceptable risk instead of a warning sign. Vaughan called it the normalization of deviance, the process by which a signal that should trigger alarm gets redefined as normal simply because nothing catastrophic happened last time{{< cite 3 "Vaughan, Diane (1996). The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA. University of Chicago Press." >}}. Each layer of management inherited a slightly more comfortable version of the same data.

## When It Works: NUMMI

Run the same mechanism in reverse and you get NUMMI, the joint venture Toyota and General Motors opened in a shuttered GM plant in Fremont, California, in 1984. That plant had been GM's worst, plagued by absenteeism, sabotage, and defect rates nobody could fix from the top down.

Toyota didn't bring new machines. It brought a different theory about where the knowledge lived. Line workers got explicit authority to stop production the moment they spotted a defect, and management's job shifted to showing up fast when they did. Within about a year, using largely the same workforce that had made GM's worst cars, the plant was producing some of the best quality in the entire GM system{{< cite 4 "Glass, Ira, and Frank Langfitt (2010). NUMMI. This American Life, Episode 403." >}}.

Nothing about the workers changed. What changed was whether their judgment counted for anything before a defect shipped.

## Common Mistakes

**Installing a suggestion box instead of authority.** Asking workers for feedback costs nothing and changes nothing if their input still has to survive three layers of approval before anyone acts on it. A suggestion box is a listening exercise. Deming was arguing for a transfer of authority.

**Confusing "closest to the work" with "always right about the fix."** Boisjoly and his colleagues knew exactly what was wrong. They didn't necessarily know how to redesign the joint. Respecting frontline knowledge means giving their diagnosis weight, not treating every proposed fix as automatically correct.

**Replacing systemic fixes with slogans.** Deming watched companies hang "zero defects" posters instead of fixing the process generating the defects, then blame workers when the posters didn't work{{< cite 2 "Deming, W. Edwards (1986). Out of the Crisis. MIT Press." >}}. A poster is cheaper than fixing a system. It is also useless.

**Rewarding the warning only after it's proven right.** Boisjoly's memo was vindicated by the disaster it predicted, which is the worst possible way for a warning to get taken seriously. An organization that only believes its frontline in hindsight has built no functioning channel at all.

## Put It Into Practice

Find the layer in your organization where a problem currently has to survive to get fixed, and count how many approvals sit between the person who saw it and the person who can act on it. Every one of those layers is a place the signal can get filtered into something more comfortable.

Then give real authority, not just a hearing, to the people who see the system daily. That means someone can say no to a launch, halt a line, or flag a defect and have it matter immediately, not after a review cycle turns it into a bullet point on someone else's slide.

The next Boisjoly in your organization is probably already writing the memo. The only open question is whether anyone with the authority to act on it is still close enough to understand what it says.

## Go Further

**Genchi genbutsu.** Toyota's own term for going to the actual place and seeing the actual thing, rather than managing from a report about it, predates and parallels Deming's argument{{< cite 5 "Ohno, Taiichi (1988). Toyota Production System: Beyond Large-Scale Production. Productivity Press." >}}.

**Psychological safety research.** Amy Edmondson's work on why some teams report errors and others hide them gives Deming's "drive out fear" an empirical, measurable foundation four decades later{{< cite 6 "Edmondson, Amy (1999). Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams. Administrative Science Quarterly, 44(2)." >}}.

**Statistical process control.** Deming's argument rests on his separate work on distinguishing normal variation from a genuine signal, the technical half of his System of Profound Knowledge that this post doesn't cover.

## Outtakes

**Deming learned this in Japan first.** American manufacturers largely ignored Deming after World War II. Japanese companies didn't, and his methods are widely credited as part of what took Japanese manufacturing from a byword for cheap goods to a global standard within a generation.

**The Rogers Commission had a dissenter of its own.** Physicist Richard Feynman famously demonstrated the O-ring problem live on television by dropping a compressed O-ring into ice water and watching it fail to spring back, a five-minute experiment that outdid months of commission testimony.

**NUMMI's plant still runs.** The Fremont factory didn't close when the joint venture ended in 2010. Tesla bought it, and builds cars on the same floor where GM's worst plant became one of its best.
