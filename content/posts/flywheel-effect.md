## The Flywheel Effect

Spring 2001. Amazon's stock has fallen more than 90 percent from its dot-com peak. The press has taken to calling it Amazon.bomb. Jeff Bezos brings in a management consultant for an executive retreat, and somewhere in that room, someone sketches a loop of circles and arrows on a napkin.

That napkin becomes the operating logic for one of the most valuable companies on the planet. Not a slogan. Not a mission statement. A loop, and a bet that spinning it faster would matter more than any single decision inside it.

## What Is the Flywheel Effect?

Jim Collins introduced the concept in Good to Great, describing a giant flywheel, a metal disk 30 feet across and 2 feet thick, weighing about 5,000 pounds{{< cite 1 "Collins, Jim (2001). Good to Great. HarperBusiness." >}}. Pushing it takes everything you have. The first rotation takes two or three hours of steady effort. The second comes a little faster. By the tenth or hundredth turn, the wheel has enough of its own momentum that the same push moves it further than it did at turn one.

Collins built the metaphor to kill a specific myth: that great companies get that way through one bold move, a single acquisition, a visionary launch, a killer product. They don't. They get there through years of turns in the same direction, each one unremarkable on its own, until the accumulated momentum looks, from the outside, like a sudden breakthrough{{< cite 1 "Collins, Jim (2001). Good to Great. HarperBusiness." >}}.

## The Napkin That Ran a Company

Bezos brought Collins in specifically to help Amazon figure out what its own wheel looked like. What came out of that retreat was a simple loop, later reconstructed in detail by Brad Stone: lower prices attract more customers, more customers attract more third-party sellers, more sellers mean more selection, more selection improves the customer experience, and a better customer experience justifies lower prices all over again{{< cite 2 "Stone, Brad (2013). The Everything Store: Jeff Bezos and the Age of Amazon. Little, Brown and Company." >}}.

None of those five links is impressive by itself. Lower prices are just lower prices. The trick was making each one feed the next, so that investment in any single link paid off across the whole loop instead of dying at the edge of its own department.

## Why Momentum Beats a Big Push

The physics underneath the metaphor is worth taking seriously, because it explains why the early turns feel so pointless. A spinning object stores kinetic energy proportional to its moment of inertia and the square of its angular velocity{{< cite 3 "Nave, Carl R. HyperPhysics: Rotational Kinetic Energy. Georgia State University." >}}. Square terms are unforgiving at low speed and generous at high speed. Double a flywheel's speed and you quadruple the energy stored in it, using the same-sized push you used at turn one.

That's the part the metaphor gets exactly right. A company's tenth year of consistent execution isn't ten times more effective than its first. It behaves like it's a hundred times more effective, because the compounding runs on the same curve. Which also means quitting at turn nine, right before the curve bends upward, is the single most common way to waste nine turns of work.

## Every Loop Needs Its Own Wheel

Amazon's wheel is not your wheel. HubSpot built its entire go-to-market model around a version tuned for marketing and sales instead of retail, replacing the traditional funnel with a loop of attract, engage, and delight, where customers who reach the end feed back in as promoters rather than disappearing as conversions{{< cite 4 "HubSpot. The Flywheel Model." >}}. The elements changed. The principle, that each stage should power the next one rather than sit there as a dead end, didn't.

Finding your own loop takes staring hard at what already compounds in your business without anyone planning it that way, then investing in the connections between the parts instead of any single part in isolation.

## Common Mistakes

**Calling any loop a flywheel.** Collecting more user data doesn't make a flywheel. Most founders assume more data automatically means a better product, which means more users, which means more data. It rarely holds, because raw volume runs into diminishing returns fast. A real flywheel needs a closed loop that converts data into a specific action, not just a bigger pile of data{{< cite 5 "Marianantoni, Alessandro (2026). Data Flywheels Are Real, But Most Founders Build Them Wrong. M Accelerator." >}}.

**Expecting turn one to look like turn one hundred.** The first push is the hardest and the least impressive. Teams that judge a new initiative by its first quarter's results are measuring the slowest, least representative part of the whole curve.

**Falling into the doom loop.** Collins names the opposite failure mode directly: companies that push the wheel one direction, stall, then lurch it a different direction entirely, chasing whatever program promises a faster breakthrough. Years of that produces no accumulated momentum at all, just a wheel that's been shoved in a dozen directions and gone nowhere in any of them{{< cite 1 "Collins, Jim (2001). Good to Great. HarperBusiness." >}}.

## Put It Into Practice

Map your own loop before you try to spin it faster. Write down the three or four things in your business that already reinforce each other, even weakly, and be honest about which connections are real and which ones you're hoping are real.

Then find the friction. Every flywheel has a link that's weaker than the rest, a place where the loop leaks momentum instead of passing it forward. Fixing that one link usually beats adding a new one.

Give it more turns than feels reasonable before you judge it. The wheel that's about to break through looks identical to the wheel that never will, right up until it doesn't.

## Go Further

**The follow-up monograph.** Collins expanded the concept into a standalone book, working through how to name your own flywheel's specific components and diagnose which one is underperforming{{< cite 1 "Collins, Jim (2001). Good to Great. HarperBusiness." >}}.

**Flywheels versus network effects.** Not every compounding loop is a flywheel in Collins's sense. Network effects, where a product gets more valuable as more people join, are a related but distinct mechanism worth telling apart from operational flywheels.

**Data flywheels in AI products.** Machine learning products often claim a flywheel where usage improves the model, which improves the product, which drives more usage. The mechanism is real but fragile, and the same volume-over-quality mistake that trips up founders elsewhere shows up here too{{< cite 5 "Marianantoni, Alessandro (2026). Data Flywheels Are Real, But Most Founders Build Them Wrong. M Accelerator." >}}.

## Outtakes

**The consultant who out-earned the company.** Jim Collins reportedly billed Amazon a flat fee for that 2001 retreat rather than equity. Given what the flywheel sketch was worth to the company over the next two decades, it may be the most underpriced afternoon of consulting in business history.

**The wheel is older than the metaphor.** Steam engines have used physical flywheels since the 1700s to smooth out the jerky power strokes of a piston into steady rotation. Collins borrowed a 250-year-old engineering trick to explain corporate strategy, and it fit almost too well.

**Egg or chicken, Collins picked neither.** Asked whether momentum or the push that built it comes first, Collins's answer is that it's the wrong question. There's no first domino, just accumulated turns. That's also why the metaphor frustrates executives hunting for a single lever to pull.
