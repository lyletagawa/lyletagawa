## The Luddites

Nottingham, March 11, 1811. A group of stocking workers breaks into a frame-knitting shop and destroys several machines with sledgehammers they call "Great Enoch." The shop owner uses the frames to produce inferior hosiery with unskilled workers at low wages, undercutting what trained craftsmen can charge for quality work.

The workers aren't against machines. They've built careers on them, and they're against being made poor by them.

Two hundred years later, someone who expresses skepticism about AI tools gets called a Luddite. It lands as an insult. As a comparison, it's more precise than most people realize.

## Not Against Technology

The textile workers who organized under the name of the mythical "Ned Ludd" between 1811 and 1816 were skilled craftsmen, not ignorant villagers. Kevin Binfield, who edited the primary collection of their writings, put it plainly: they "were totally fine with machines"{{< cite 1 "Binfield, Kevin, ed. (2004). Writings of the Luddites. University Press of Kentucky. https://www.smithsonianmag.com/history/what-the-luddites-really-fought-against-264412/" >}}. Their actual complaints were about wages, labor standards, and manufacturers using machinery "in a fraudulent and deceitful manner" to bypass the apprenticeship system that kept quality high and pay fair.

The name "Ned Ludd" was fictional, probably invented to protect organizers{{< cite 2 "\"Luddite.\" Wikipedia. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luddite" >}}. The Luddites used it as a pseudonym in threatening letters to mill owners. The organization behind the name was real. Coordinated raids swept across Nottinghamshire and Yorkshire, timed for night, targeting specific machines owned by specific owners who had broken with fair labor practices.

The British government took it seriously. Parliament deployed 12,000 troops{{< cite 2 "\"Luddite.\" Wikipedia. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luddite" >}}, more than Wellington commanded in the Peninsular War at the time. The Frame Breaking Act of 1812 made machine-breaking a capital offense. Of the more than 60 men tried at the York trials in January 1813, three were hanged for the murder of mill owner William Horsfall and dozens more were transported to Australia.

## Same Grievance, New Machines

The people who get called Luddites today are making a version of the same argument. AI is being used not just to augment human work but to reduce what employers pay for it.

On Upwork, one of the largest freelancing platforms, workers in AI-exposed occupations saw a 2 percent decline in monthly contracts and a 5.2 percent drop in monthly earnings following ChatGPT's release{{< cite 3 "Hui, Xiang, Oren Reshef, and Luofeng Zhou (2024). \"The Short-Term Effects of Generative Artificial Intelligence on Employment: Evidence from an Online Labor Market.\" Organization Science, 35(6): 1977-1989. https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.2023.18441" >}}. Writing-related job posts fell 21 percent within eight months. The workers hit hardest weren't beginners. They were experienced freelancers with the best track records and highest rates. AI compressed their advantage into something any client could buy cheaply.

This is what the stocking workers of 1811 were describing. Power looms produced cloth faster. The cloth cost less, the weavers earned less, and the mill owner captured the gain.

Klarna cut approximately 700 customer service jobs between 2022 and 2024 and replaced them with an AI assistant the company claimed could do the work of 853 full-time employees{{< cite 4 "Klarna CEO admits aggressive AI job cuts went too far, starts hiring again. MLQ News (2025). https://mlq.ai/news/klarna-ceo-admits-aggressive-ai-job-cuts-went-too-far-starts-hiring-again-after-us-ipo/" >}}. Customer satisfaction fell. In 2025, the CEO said "We went too far." The company started rehiring. The 700 people who lost those jobs did not get them back.

In 2023, the Writers Guild of America (WGA) struck for 148 days, with nearly 11,500 writers walking out{{< cite 5 "Writers' Guild of America WGA Strike Resolution: AI Restrictions and Implications. Fordham Journal of Corporate and Financial Law (2023). https://news.law.fordham.edu/jcfl/2023/10/25/writers-guild-of-america-wga-strike-resolution-ai-restrictions-and-implications/" >}}. They demanded that AI-generated material not be called a script, and that writers not be forced to incorporate AI output into their work. They won those protections. The Luddites couldn't negotiate like this. Collective bargaining was illegal in 1811.

## What the Luddites Got Wrong

**Machine-breaking doesn't work.** Smashing a frame was a one-night setback for a mill owner and a multi-year legal liability for the worker who did it. Machines are cheap to replace. The structural economics that made them attractive don't change.

**They organized too late.** By 1811, the mechanization of textile production was already well underway. The Luddites had leverage only while skilled workers remained necessary. Organizing after displacement had begun meant negotiating from a weakened position.

**The machine was the wrong target.** Manufacturers chose to use machines to cut wages. That was a business decision, not a technological inevitability. Other manufacturers paid fair wages and used the same machines. The problem wasn't the frame. It was the race to the bottom that followed its adoption.

**Violence handed the government an excuse.** The mill attacks, and especially the murder of William Horsfall, gave Parliament cover to treat the movement as a criminal conspiracy rather than a labor dispute. The 12,000 troops were already on their way, but the murder made the outcome inevitable.

## About That Long Run

The standard rebuttal to Luddite concerns is that technology always creates more jobs than it destroys. The Industrial Revolution is the usual evidence. And it's true, eventually.

Between 1840 and 1900, output per worker rose 90 percent and real wages rose 123 percent{{< cite 6 "Allen, Robert C. (2009). \"Engels' pause: Technical change, capital accumulation, and inequality in the British industrial revolution.\" Explorations in Economic History, 46(4): 418-435. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0014498309000199" >}}. Workers' lives improved. The optimists have the historical record.

What the optimists omit is the 50 years before 1840. Economic historian Robert Allen named this gap "Engels' pause" after Friedrich Engels, who documented the conditions firsthand. From 1790 to 1840, productivity grew 46 percent while real wages grew 12 percent. Capital's income share doubled. The workers smashing frames in 1811 were not imagining their deteriorating conditions. They were living in them.

Whether AI follows the same arc is contested. Daron Acemoglu, who shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Economics, estimates that AI will add roughly 0.06 percent annually to total factor productivity growth{{< cite 7 "Acemoglu, Daron (2024). What do we know about the economics of AI? MIT Economics. https://economics.mit.edu/news/daron-acemoglu-what-do-we-know-about-economics-ai" >}}. He argues that AI designed to replace workers rather than complement them won't distribute gains the way past automation eventually did.

The long run is only comforting if you're not the generation doing the adjusting.

## Conclusion

Calling someone a Luddite is supposed to be an insult. The implication is technological illiteracy, fear of progress, an inability to see the obvious benefits. But the original Luddites understood the mechanics of their situation clearly. They knew who was capturing the gains from automation. They knew that cheaper production didn't automatically mean better wages. They were right about all of it, and they were crushed anyway.

The modern version of this question isn't going away. Whether AI-driven productivity gains flow to workers or to the owners of the AI tools is a policy question, not a technological inevitability. It got settled the last time by 12,000 troops and a capital offense statute, in favor of the mill owners. Today's workers have legal tools the Luddites never had. You can organize, negotiate, and put AI restrictions in a contract. The WGA did.

You probably won't need to smash anything.

## Outtakes

**Great Enoch.** The sledgehammers used to smash stocking frames were nicknamed "Great Enoch" after Enoch Taylor, a local manufacturer who made both the frames and the hammers used to break them. "Enoch makes them, Enoch breaks them" became a rallying cry. Taylor himself was never a target.
